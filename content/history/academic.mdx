## Prexy’s Moon

Among the many traditions and stories tied to the Academic Building at Texas A&M University, “Prexy’s Moon” stands out as a small but memorable feature that once quite literally shone over campus life.

### What Was “Prexy’s Moon”?

“Prexy’s Moon” was a **small light mounted atop the dome of the Academic Building** in the years following its completion in 1914. The name comes from the slang term *“prexy,”* a common nickname for the college president, paired with “moon,” reflecting the lamp’s glow over campus at night.

Functionally, it served a simple purpose:

* It **illuminated the campus after dark**, helping students—many of them cadets—navigate their way across the grounds during late hours.

At a time when campus lighting was limited, this small beacon became both **practical and symbolic**, marking the Academic Building as a central point of orientation.

### A Short-Lived Feature

Despite its usefulness, Prexy’s Moon did not last long. It was **removed in 1931**, less than two decades after the Academic Building opened.

The reason reflects a very different era of student life:

* After **World War I**, many cadets had familiarity with firearms and, in some cases, access to ammunition.
* With relatively few organized weekend activities, informal shooting contests became a pastime.
* The light atop the dome became an appealing target.

Repeated damage from gunfire led to constant repairs, and eventually the administration chose to remove the fixture altogether rather than continue maintaining it.

### Where Is It Now?

Though no longer atop the dome, Prexy’s Moon still exists.
It is preserved today in the **Mayo-Thomas Room of Cushing Memorial Library & Archives**, where it remains a tangible artifact of early 20th-century campus life.

### The Academic Building Context

Prexy’s Moon is just one part of the rich history surrounding the Academic Building:

* The building was constructed **in 1914** on the site of **Old Main**, which was destroyed by fire in 1912.
* Designed under the leadership of **Dr. Frederick Giesecke**, it became a defining architectural and symbolic centerpiece of campus.
* Its distinctive **copper dome**—once topped by Prexy’s Moon—remains one of the most recognizable features at Texas A&M.

Over time, the building has accumulated layers of meaning:

* It houses the **Texas A&M University seal mosaic** in its foyer
* It contains a **replica of the Liberty Bell**, placed there in 1950
* It serves as the focal point for **Silver Taps**, one of the university’s most solemn traditions

### Significance

Prexy’s Moon may have been a small fixture, but it reflects larger themes in Texas A&M’s history:

* The evolution of campus infrastructure (from minimal lighting to a modern university)
* The character of early cadet life, including informal traditions and pastimes
* The way practical features can become enduring stories

Today, Prexy’s Moon is remembered less for the light it cast and more for the glimpse it provides into a different era—when the Academic Building quite literally watched over campus, and students occasionally took aim at it.
